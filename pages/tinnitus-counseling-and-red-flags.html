Tinnitus Counseling and Red Flags

Original counseling handoutPermission: host original textLast reviewed: May 11, 2026

Intended audience: Audiologists, ENT partners, trainees

Patient population: Adults or teens reporting tinnitus

Clinical purpose: Provide a safe first counseling handout.

Key counseling points

  • Tinnitus is the perception of sound without an external sound source. It may be linked with hearing loss, noise exposure, medication effects, ear disease, stress, sleep disruption, or other health factors (National Institute for Health and Care Excellence, 2020) (American Academy of Otolaryngology Head and Neck Surgery Foundation, 2014).
  • Helpful management may include education, hearing assessment, hearing aids when hearing loss is present, sound enrichment, sleep strategies, and referral for psychological support when distress is significant (National Institute for Health and Care Excellence, 2020) (National Center for Rehabilitative Auditory Research, 2024) (American Academy of Otolaryngology Head and Neck Surgery Foundation, 2014).
  • The goal is not to promise silence. The goal is to reduce distress, improve coping, support sleep, and address treatable contributors (National Institute for Health and Care Excellence, 2020) (National Center for Rehabilitative Auditory Research, 2024).

Clinician notes

Use at tinnitus intake and follow-up. Consider THI, TFI, sleep screening, anxiety/depression screening pathway, and medical red flags.

Red flags and referral indicators

Refer urgently or medically when tinnitus is pulsatile, unilateral with asymmetric hearing concerns, sudden onset with hearing loss, associated with neurologic signs, severe distress, suicidality, pain, drainage, or vertigo (National Institute for Health and Care Excellence, 2020) (American Academy of Otolaryngology Head and Neck Surgery Foundation, 2014) (U.S. Food and Drug Administration, 2022).
