Usher Syndrome

Original patient education handoutPrintable PDFLast reviewed: May 11, 2026

What this handout explains: Usher syndrome is a genetic condition involving hearing loss and progressive vision loss from retinitis pigmentosa. Some types also include balance differences.

Patient message: Genetic information can affect the patient and family. Consider genetic counseling or medical genetics referral when recommended by the care team.

Facts at a glance

  • Genetic hearing loss may be syndromic, meaning hearing loss occurs with findings in other body systems (Shearer et al., 2025) (GeneReviews, 2026).
  • Children with hearing loss need hearing access and intervention planning that supports communication and development (JCIH, 2019).
  • Vision changes can affect communication access, orientation, mobility, and device use, so coordinated care is important (Shearer et al., 2025) .

Hearing, vision, and balance

  • Hearing loss may be present from childhood or vary by type.
  • Vision changes usually relate to progressive retinal disease.
  • Balance differences may occur in some types.
  • The combination of hearing and vision changes can affect communication in ways that hearing care alone cannot solve.

Audiology care

  • Fit and verify hearing devices when appropriate.
  • Discuss cochlear implant referral when hearing aids provide limited benefit.
  • Coordinate with low-vision, ophthalmology, orientation and mobility, educational, and genetics teams.

Communication planning

  • Use lighting, seating, captions, tactile cues, written supports, and assistive technology.
  • Plan for communication needs as vision changes.
  • Support self-advocacy in school, work, and healthcare settings.

When to seek help

  • Sudden hearing change, sudden vision change, falls, new dizziness, or rapid change in device benefit should be reported.

Take-home message

Usher syndrome care should anticipate both hearing and vision access needs.
