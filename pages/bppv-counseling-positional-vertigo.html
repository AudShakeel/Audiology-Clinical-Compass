BPPV Counseling: Positional Vertigo

Original counseling handoutPermission: host original textLast reviewed: May 11, 2026

Intended audience: Vestibular audiologists, ENT teams, patients

Patient population: Adults with suspected or diagnosed BPPV

Clinical purpose: Explain BPPV care and safety.

Original patient counseling visual. It does not replace vestibular testing or medical evaluation.

Key counseling points

  • BPPV usually causes brief spinning vertigo triggered by position changes such as lying down, rolling over, or looking up (American Academy of Otolaryngology Head and Neck Surgery Foundation, 2017) (Bhattacharyya et al., 2017).
  • Evidence-based care emphasizes accurate positional diagnosis, appropriate canalith repositioning maneuvers, and avoiding unnecessary imaging or vestibular suppressants when the presentation is typical (American Academy of Otolaryngology Head and Neck Surgery Foundation, 2017) (Bhattacharyya et al., 2017).
  • Patients should be counseled about fall safety, recurrence, follow-up when symptoms persist, and medical review when symptoms do not fit typical BPPV (American Academy of Otolaryngology Head and Neck Surgery Foundation, 2017).

Clinician notes

Use after positional testing or referral. Do not provide maneuver instructions unless the clinician has evaluated safety and canal involvement.

Red flags and referral indicators

Red flags include new neurologic symptoms, severe headache, fainting, new hearing loss, continuous vertigo, inability to walk, chest pain, or atypical nystagmus (American Academy of Otolaryngology Head and Neck Surgery Foundation, 2017).

What happens next

Refer to ENT, neurology, emergency care, or vestibular therapy based on red flags and local protocol.
