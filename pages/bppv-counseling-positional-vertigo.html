BPPV Counseling: Positional Vertigo

Original patient education handoutPrintable PDFLast reviewed: May 11, 2026

What this handout explains: BPPV is a common cause of brief spinning vertigo triggered by certain head positions. It is often related to tiny inner-ear crystals moving into a semicircular canal.

Patient message: Do not drive, climb, or perform unsafe tasks during active vertigo. Follow the safety advice from your clinician.

Facts at a glance

  • The BPPV guideline aims to improve accurate diagnosis, reduce unnecessary imaging or vestibular suppressant use, and increase appropriate repositioning maneuvers (AAO-HNSF, 2017) (Bhattacharyya et al., 2017).
  • Typical BPPV causes brief positional vertigo, often with rolling in bed, looking up, bending, or turning the head (AAO-HNSF, 2017).
  • Not all dizziness is BPPV, and red flags should be medically evaluated (AAO-HNSF, 2017) .

What evaluation may include

  • History of triggers, duration, falls, neurologic symptoms, migraine, hearing symptoms, and medications.
  • Positional testing when safe and appropriate.
  • Canalith repositioning treatment when findings match BPPV and no contraindication is present (AAO-HNSF, 2017) (Bhattacharyya et al., 2017).

What may happen after treatment

  • Symptoms may improve quickly, but some people feel mildly off-balance for a short time.
  • BPPV can recur.
  • Your clinician may give instructions about activity, safety, or follow-up based on your case.

Red flags

  • New weakness, numbness, facial droop, trouble speaking, severe headache, chest pain, fainting, or inability to walk safely.
  • Continuous vertigo that does not fit the brief positional pattern.
  • New sudden hearing loss or neurologic symptoms.
  • These symptoms require urgent medical guidance (AAO-HNSF, 2017) (NICE, 2023).

Questions to ask

  • Which canal or side was involved?
  • Was a repositioning maneuver performed?
  • What should I do if symptoms come back?
  • Do I need vestibular rehabilitation or medical follow-up?

Take-home message

BPPV is often treatable, but dizziness should be evaluated carefully when the pattern is not typical.
