Hyperacusis: Sound Sensitivity Counseling

Original patient education handoutPrintable PDFLast reviewed: May 11, 2026

What this handout explains: Hyperacusis is reduced tolerance to everyday sounds. The sound may feel too loud, painful, startling, or overwhelming even when other people find it acceptable.

Patient message: The goal is not to force exposure to painful sound. The goal is to protect from hazardous sound while gradually rebuilding tolerance to safe everyday sound.

Facts at a glance

  • Sound tolerance concerns require careful history, hearing evaluation when appropriate, counseling, and referral when symptoms suggest medical or mental health needs (NICE, 2020) (AAO-HNSF, 2014).
  • Overuse of sound avoidance can sometimes make normal sound feel even harder to tolerate, so plans should be individualized and gradual (NCRAR, 2024).
  • Ear pain, sudden hearing change, drainage, dizziness, or severe distress should be reviewed rather than treated as routine sensitivity (FDA, 2022) (NICE, 2020) .

What to notice

  • Which sounds are painful, startling, or emotionally overwhelming.
  • Whether symptoms are loudness-based, fear-based, pain-based, or connected to specific triggers.
  • Whether hearing protection is used only in unsafe noise or also in quiet everyday places.

What can help

  • Use hearing protection for truly loud sound, such as power tools, concerts, firearms, or industrial noise.
  • Avoid wearing earplugs all day in safe environments unless a clinician recommends it for a specific reason.
  • Sound therapy or gradual sound enrichment may be considered under clinician guidance (NCRAR, 2024) (NICE, 2020).
  • Counseling, anxiety support, occupational planning, and environmental modifications may be needed.

When to get help

  • Get medical or clinical guidance for sudden hearing change, ear pain, drainage, vertigo, facial weakness, severe headache, or sound sensitivity after trauma (FDA, 2022) (NICE, 2020).

Questions to ask

  • Which sounds are unsafe versus uncomfortable?
  • How should I use hearing protection without overprotecting?
  • Should tinnitus, migraine, anxiety, autism, concussion, or other factors be considered?

Take-home message

Safe sound protection and gradual tolerance building are different. A good plan separates the two.
