Hyperacusis: Sound Sensitivity Counseling

Original counseling handoutPermission: host original textLast reviewed: May 11, 2026

Intended audience: Audiologists and tinnitus clinicians

Patient population: Patients reporting reduced sound tolerance

Clinical purpose: Give cautious counseling about sound sensitivity.

Original counseling visual based on public health noise guidance.

Key counseling points

  • Hyperacusis describes reduced tolerance for everyday sound. It can overlap with tinnitus, migraine, anxiety, noise exposure history, and other auditory or medical concerns (National Institute for Health and Care Excellence, 2020) (American Academy of Otolaryngology Head and Neck Surgery Foundation, 2014).
  • Counseling commonly emphasizes safety, avoiding harmful sound, reducing unnecessary overprotection, and gradual re-engagement with comfortable daily sound when medically appropriate (National Institute for Health and Care Excellence, 2020) (National Center for Rehabilitative Auditory Research, 2024).
  • Treatment should be individualized. Patients with pain, sudden onset, neurologic symptoms, or severe functional limitation need coordinated medical or mental health referral (National Institute for Health and Care Excellence, 2020) (U.S. Food and Drug Administration, 2022).

Clinician notes

Use as educational support. Avoid claiming a single cure. Consider loudness discomfort levels only when clinically appropriate and interpreted cautiously.

Red flags and referral indicators

Red flags include sound-triggered severe pain, unilateral sudden symptoms, dizziness, neurologic signs, suicidal thoughts, or inability to function safely (National Institute for Health and Care Excellence, 2020) (U.S. Food and Drug Administration, 2022).

What happens next

Coordinate audiology, ENT, psychology, occupational therapy, migraine care, or pain specialists when indicated.
