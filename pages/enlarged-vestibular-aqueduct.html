Enlarged Vestibular Aqueduct

Original patient education handoutPrintable PDFLast reviewed: May 11, 2026

What this handout explains: Enlarged vestibular aqueduct is an inner-ear finding associated with childhood hearing loss. Hearing can be stable, fluctuating, or progressive.

Patient message: Families should ask the medical team about activity precautions, head injury risk, genetic counseling, and follow-up. Advice should come from the treating medical team.

Facts at a glance

  • NIDCD describes enlarged vestibular aqueducts as inner-ear differences associated with childhood hearing loss (NIDCD, 2017).
  • EVA can occur with Pendred syndrome or other genetic hearing-loss patterns (NIDCD, 2017) (Shearer et al., 2025).
  • Children with hearing loss need timely intervention, monitoring, and family-centered support (JCIH, 2019) .

Hearing features

  • Hearing loss may be sensorineural or mixed depending on the full findings.
  • Hearing may change over time or after certain events.
  • One or both ears may be affected.

Clinical follow-up

  • Serial audiograms.
  • Hearing technology review.
  • School support and communication planning.
  • Medical imaging or genetic testing discussion when recommended by ENT or genetics.

What families can track

  • Changes after falls, head injury, illness, or pressure-related events.
  • New dizziness, imbalance, or tinnitus.
  • Changes in school listening, speech, or fatigue.

When to seek help

  • Sudden hearing change, new severe dizziness, head injury with hearing change, or device benefit suddenly worsening should be reported quickly.

Take-home message

EVA requires monitoring because hearing may change and early support helps protect communication access.
