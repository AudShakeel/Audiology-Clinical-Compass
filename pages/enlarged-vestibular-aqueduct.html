Enlarged Vestibular Aqueduct

Original counseling handoutPermission: host original textLast reviewed: May 11, 2026

Intended audience: Audiologists, families, students

Patient population: Patients with diagnosed or suspected genetic, syndromic, or congenital hearing and balance concerns

Clinical purpose: Provide a concise patient education handout for Enlarged Vestibular Aqueduct.

Key counseling points

  • EVA is an inner-ear imaging finding that can be associated with childhood sensorineural hearing loss and may point to an underlying genetic cause. (National Institute on Deafness and Other Communication Disorders, 2017) (National Institute on Deafness and Other Communication Disorders, 2017) (Shearer et al., 2025) (Joint Committee on Infant Hearing, 2019).
  • Hearing loss may fluctuate or progress. Families should be counseled that hearing can change and that consistent monitoring matters. (National Institute on Deafness and Other Communication Disorders, 2017) (National Institute on Deafness and Other Communication Disorders, 2017) (Shearer et al., 2025) (Joint Committee on Infant Hearing, 2019).
  • Counsel on follow-up audiology, ENT evaluation, genetic counseling when appropriate, school access, hearing technology, and safety planning for balance concerns. (National Institute on Deafness and Other Communication Disorders, 2017) (National Institute on Deafness and Other Communication Disorders, 2017) (Shearer et al., 2025) (Joint Committee on Infant Hearing, 2019).

Clinician notes

Use as a starting education sheet only. Confirm diagnosis-specific counseling with medical genetics, ENT, and the responsible medical team. Avoid implying that all patients with the syndrome have the same course.

Red flags and referral indicators

Red flags include sudden hearing change, severe dizziness, neurologic symptoms, cardiac symptoms, vision concerns, kidney concerns, developmental regression, or any acute medical symptom. Refer based on diagnosis and local protocol (U.S. Food and Drug Administration, 2022) (Shearer et al., 2025).

What happens next

Document diagnosis source, family questions, communication plan, device plan, referral status, and follow-up interval.
