Vestibular Rehabilitation: What Patients Should Know

Original patient education handoutPrintable PDFLast reviewed: May 11, 2026

What this handout explains: Vestibular rehabilitation uses individualized exercises and activity plans to help the brain and body adapt to balance and dizziness problems.

Patient message: Exercises should be uncomfortable enough to train the system but not unsafe. Follow the plan from your vestibular clinician.

Facts at a glance

  • Vestibular symptoms can affect daily activity, confidence, and participation, so outcome measures may be used to track change (Jacobson & Newman, 1990) (Powell & Myers, 1995) (Morris et al., 2009).
  • Different dizziness patterns require different plans. BPPV, PPPD, vestibular migraine, and unilateral vestibular loss are not treated the same way (AAO-HNSF, 2017) (Staab et al., 2017) (Lempert et al., 2022).
  • Functional gait and balance tools can help guide rehabilitation and fall-risk planning (Wrisley et al., 2004) (Powell & Myers, 1995) .

What therapy may target

  • Gaze stability.
  • Balance and walking confidence.
  • Motion sensitivity.
  • Visual motion tolerance.
  • Return to work, school, driving, exercise, or daily activities.

What helps progress

  • Do exercises as prescribed, not all at once.
  • Track symptoms, falls, near-falls, and activities that are improving.
  • Avoid unnecessary avoidance, but respect safety limits.
  • Use assistive devices or fall precautions when recommended.

When to stop and call

  • New neurologic symptoms.
  • Fainting, chest pain, severe headache, or new weakness.
  • Sudden hearing loss.
  • Falls, injury, or symptoms that feel unsafe.

Questions to ask

  • What diagnosis is the exercise plan targeting?
  • How much symptom increase is acceptable?
  • How will progress be measured?
  • Should DHI, ABC Scale, FGA, VAP, or another measure be used from an official source?

Take-home message

Vestibular rehabilitation should be individualized, measurable, and safety-focused.
