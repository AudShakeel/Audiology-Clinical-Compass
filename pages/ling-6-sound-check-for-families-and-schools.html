Ling 6 Sound Check for Families and Schools

Original counseling handoutPermission: host original textLast reviewed: May 11, 2026

Intended audience: Pediatric clinicians, families, teachers

Patient population: Children using hearing aids, CIs, bone-conduction devices, or remote microphones

Clinical purpose: Provide a simple listening access check.

Key counseling points

  • A daily listening check helps identify device, battery, microphone, earmold, mapping, or access problems before the child misses important listening opportunities (American Academy of Audiology, 2013) (Joint Committee on Infant Hearing, 2019).
  • The check is not a full hearing test. It is a functional screen that can show whether the child detects a range of speech sounds with the usual device setup (American Academy of Audiology, 2013).
  • Families and teachers should report inconsistent responses, new errors, device intermittency, or reduced progress to the audiology team (Joint Committee on Infant Hearing, 2019) (American Academy of Audiology, 2013).

Clinician notes

Do not reproduce restricted branded forms. Create clinic-specific instructions and cite clinical sources.

Red flags and referral indicators

Sudden loss of response, ear pain, drainage, device site problems, or vestibular symptoms should prompt clinical review (Joint Committee on Infant Hearing, 2019) (U.S. Food and Drug Administration, 2022).

What happens next

Document distance, device condition, child response, and follow-up plan.
