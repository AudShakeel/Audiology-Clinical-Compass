Ling 6 Sound Check for Families and Schools

Original patient education handoutPrintable PDFLast reviewed: May 11, 2026

What this handout explains: The Ling-6 sound check is a quick listening check used to see whether a child detects a range of speech sounds through hearing devices. It does not replace a hearing test.

Patient message: Do the check in a quiet place. Present sounds in a natural voice and avoid giving visual cues unless your clinician teaches you to do so.

Facts at a glance

  • Children with hearing loss need consistent monitoring of access to speech and device function (JCIH, 2019) (American Academy of Audiology, 2013).
  • Pediatric assessment and follow-up often combine clinical test results with functional listening observations from families and schools (American Academy of Audiology, 2020).
  • A daily listening check can help identify device problems before a child misses a day of access (American Academy of Audiology, 2013) .

The six sounds

  • /m/ as in moon.
  • /ah/ as in father.
  • /oo/ as in boot.
  • /ee/ as in see.
  • /sh/ as in shoe.
  • /s/ as in sun.

How to use it

  • Check each device condition recommended by your audiologist, such as right, left, both, HA, CI, or remote microphone.
  • Stand at the distance recommended by your clinic or school team.
  • Record whether the child detects, imitates, points, or identifies the sound.
  • Repeat if the child is distracted, tired, or not ready.

What results mean

  • A missed sound may be caused by device problems, wax, distance, noise, attention, fatigue, or hearing change.
  • One missed check does not diagnose hearing change, but repeated misses should be reported.
  • Clinicians may use formal testing to verify audibility and device function (American Academy of Audiology, 2020) (American Academy of Audiology, 2013).

When to call the clinic or school team

  • A sound that was usually heard is repeatedly missed.
  • One device seems weaker.
  • Feedback, intermittent sound, or device rejection increases.
  • The child reports pain, dizziness, or sudden change.

Take-home message

A daily listening check is a simple way to protect access to speech.
