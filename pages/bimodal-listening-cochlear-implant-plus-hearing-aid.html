Bimodal Listening: Cochlear Implant Plus Hearing Aid

Original patient education handoutPrintable PDFLast reviewed: May 11, 2026

What this handout explains: Bimodal listening means using a cochlear implant in one ear and a hearing aid in the other ear. The goal is to give the brain useful information from both sides when appropriate.

Patient message: Bimodal listening may feel unbalanced at first. The CI and HA provide different kinds of information, and the brain may need time and practice to combine them.

Facts at a glance

  • Adult CI referral and candidacy decisions consider aided speech understanding and benefit from appropriately fit hearing aids (Zeitler et al., 2023) (NICE, 2019).
  • The hearing aid side should be evaluated and fit appropriately, not ignored, when it provides usable access (ASHA, 2024) (APSO, 2021).
  • Bimodal balance may require follow-up because acoustic and electric hearing can sound different (Zeitler et al., 2023) (ASHA, 2024) .

Why use both devices

  • The hearing aid may provide low-pitch sound, music cues, voice quality, or environmental awareness.
  • The cochlear implant may provide improved access to speech when hearing aids alone do not provide enough clarity.
  • Using both ears may support listening access when each device provides useful information (Zeitler et al., 2023) (ASHA, 2024).

Common early concerns

  • The CI sounds robotic and the hearing aid sounds muffled.
  • One side sounds louder or closer than the other.
  • Music may sound different from memory.
  • Localization may feel difficult because timing, loudness, and pitch cues are different.

What your audiologist may review

  • Hearing aid verification, acoustic coupling, loudness balance, CI mapping, datalogging, and aided speech testing can help guide changes (ASHA, 2024) (APSO, 2021) (Zeitler et al., 2023).
  • Specific programming may review MPO, high-level gain, soft-input access, frequency lowering, compression, and CI comfort levels when clinically appropriate.
  • Follow-up should use both patient goals and objective aided measures when possible.

When to call the clinic

  • One device causes pain, dizziness, facial twitching, or skin irritation.
  • Sound suddenly changes, cuts out, or becomes distorted.
  • The hearing aid or processor cannot be worn consistently because of comfort or sound quality.

Take-home message

Bimodal success usually requires fitting both sides as a system, not treating the devices separately.
