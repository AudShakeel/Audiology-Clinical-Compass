Stickler Syndrome

Original patient education handoutPrintable PDFLast reviewed: May 11, 2026

What this handout explains: Stickler syndrome is a genetic connective-tissue condition that can involve hearing loss, vision concerns, joint findings, and craniofacial differences.

Patient message: Genetic information can affect the patient and family. Consider genetic counseling or medical genetics referral when recommended by the care team.

Facts at a glance

  • Syndromic hearing loss can occur with vision, craniofacial, skeletal, or other findings (Shearer et al., 2025) (GeneReviews, 2026).
  • Children with hearing loss need monitoring and communication access planning (JCIH, 2019).
  • Hearing loss type and degree may vary, so audiologic monitoring should be individualized (Shearer et al., 2025) .

Possible features

  • Sensorineural, conductive, or mixed hearing loss depending on the person.
  • High myopia, retinal concerns, or other eye findings.
  • Cleft palate or craniofacial differences in some patients.
  • Joint pain, early arthritis, or skeletal features.

Audiology care

  • Baseline and follow-up audiograms.
  • Middle-ear monitoring when history suggests risk.
  • Hearing technology and school accommodations when needed.
  • Coordination with ophthalmology, genetics, ENT, pediatrics, craniofacial, and therapy teams.

Daily support

  • Use clear communication strategies.
  • Plan school accommodations if listening is affected.
  • Share hearing results with the broader care team.

When to seek help

  • Sudden hearing or vision change, ear pain or drainage, new balance concerns, or school performance changes should be reviewed.

Take-home message

Stickler syndrome care should protect both hearing and vision access.
