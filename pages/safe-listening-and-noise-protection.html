Safe Listening and Noise Protection

Original counseling handoutPermission: host original textLast reviewed: May 11, 2026

Intended audience: Audiologists, students, workers, musicians

Patient population: Patients exposed to recreational or occupational noise

Clinical purpose: Provide concise prevention counseling.

Original counseling visual based on public health noise guidance.

Key counseling points

  • Noise risk depends on both loudness and exposure time. NIOSH recommends an occupational exposure limit of 85 dBA as an 8-hour time-weighted average (National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health, 2024).
  • Noise-induced hearing loss can occur from repeated loud exposure or a single intense sound. Prevention includes lowering volume, increasing distance, taking quiet breaks, and using well-fit hearing protection (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, 2026) (National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health, 2024).
  • Workers exposed at or above hazardous levels need workplace hearing conservation processes consistent with occupational rules and local policy (Occupational Safety and Health Administration, 2024) (National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health, 2024).

Clinician notes

Use with industrial, musician, school, and recreational noise counseling. Do not substitute for OSHA compliance review.

Red flags and referral indicators

Refer for sudden hearing change, persistent tinnitus after exposure, asymmetric symptoms, pain, dizziness, or suspected acoustic trauma (U.S. Food and Drug Administration, 2022) (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, 2026).

What happens next

Consider audiogram monitoring, hearing protection fit training, musician plugs, custom plugs, or occupational referral.
