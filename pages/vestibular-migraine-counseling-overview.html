Vestibular Migraine: Counseling Overview

Original patient education handoutPrintable PDFLast reviewed: May 11, 2026

What this handout explains: Vestibular migraine can cause episodes of dizziness, vertigo, motion sensitivity, imbalance, or visually triggered dizziness in people with migraine features.

Patient message: Migraine-related dizziness can occur with or without headache.

Facts at a glance

  • Vestibular migraine diagnostic criteria include vestibular symptoms, migraine history, migraine features with episodes, and exclusion of other causes (Lempert et al., 2022).
  • Meniere disease, BPPV, PPPD, medication effects, and neurologic conditions can overlap with vestibular migraine symptoms (AAO-HNSF, 2020) (AAO-HNSF, 2017) (Staab et al., 2017).
  • Audiology and vestibular testing may help document hearing and balance patterns but do not replace medical diagnosis (Lempert et al., 2022) .

Symptoms people may describe

  • Spinning, rocking, swaying, or motion intolerance.
  • Sensitivity to busy visual environments, screens, stores, or driving.
  • Light, sound, smell, or headache features.
  • Episodes lasting minutes to days, depending on the person.

What helps the appointment

  • Track date, duration, triggers, headache features, hearing symptoms, tinnitus, aural fullness, medications, sleep, food, menstrual cycle, stress, and visual triggers.
  • Bring a medication list and prior audiograms or vestibular reports.
  • Tell the clinician if falls, neurologic symptoms, or sudden hearing changes occurred.

Possible care plan components

  • Medical migraine management through the appropriate provider.
  • Vestibular rehabilitation when indicated.
  • Hearing and tinnitus management if auditory symptoms are present.
  • Lifestyle and trigger review directed by the medical team.

Urgent symptoms

  • New neurologic symptoms, severe sudden headache, fainting, chest pain, inability to walk, or sudden hearing loss require urgent medical guidance.

Take-home message

Vestibular migraine is diagnosed by pattern and exclusion. Tracking symptoms helps the clinical team.
