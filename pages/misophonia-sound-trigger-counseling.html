Misophonia: Sound Trigger Counseling

Original counseling handoutPermission: host original textLast reviewed: May 11, 2026

Intended audience: Audiologists and interdisciplinary teams

Patient population: Patients with strong emotional reactions to specific sounds

Clinical purpose: Clarify misophonia versus loudness intolerance.

Key counseling points

  • Misophonia usually involves strong negative emotional or physiological reactions to specific pattern-based sounds. It is different from reduced loudness tolerance, although overlap can occur (National Institute for Health and Care Excellence, 2020).
  • Audiology counseling should validate the concern, screen for hearing and tinnitus issues, avoid overpromising device-based treatment, and coordinate with mental health professionals when distress or avoidance is significant (National Institute for Health and Care Excellence, 2020) (National Center for Rehabilitative Auditory Research, 2024).
  • Sound enrichment, coping plans, environmental planning, and interdisciplinary care may support functioning, but care should be individualized and monitored (National Institute for Health and Care Excellence, 2020).

Clinician notes

Use only as general education. Do not present as a diagnostic psychiatric tool.

Red flags and referral indicators

Severe distress, self-harm risk, aggression risk, school/work failure, or neurologic symptoms require appropriate referral (National Institute for Health and Care Excellence, 2020).

What happens next

Consider referral to psychology, psychiatry, occupational therapy, ENT, or neurology based on presentation.
