Misophonia: Sound Trigger Counseling

Original patient education handoutPrintable PDFLast reviewed: May 11, 2026

What this handout explains: Misophonia involves strong negative reactions to specific trigger sounds, often human pattern sounds such as chewing, breathing, tapping, or repetitive noises.

Patient message: The reaction can feel automatic and intense. The goal is to reduce distress and improve participation, not to tell the patient to "just ignore it."

Facts at a glance

  • Misophonia is different from hearing loss, tinnitus, and simple loudness discomfort, so counseling should separate the trigger pattern from loudness level (NICE, 2020) (NCRAR, 2024).
  • Management often needs interdisciplinary support when triggers cause significant distress, avoidance, family conflict, or school or work disruption (NCRAR, 2024).
  • Audiology can help rule out hearing concerns and guide safe sound and coping strategies without copying restricted treatment protocols (NICE, 2020) .

What to track

  • The exact trigger sounds.
  • Where they happen, who produces them, and whether visual cues make them stronger.
  • The reaction: anger, disgust, panic, pain, avoidance, or shutdown.
  • How school, work, family meals, sleep, or relationships are affected.

What may help

  • Plan predictable breaks from triggers.
  • Use neutral background sound when appropriate.
  • Develop communication scripts for family or school.
  • Consider mental health referral when distress, anxiety, anger, or avoidance is significant.
  • Avoid unsafe noise exposure and avoid using hearing protection in ways that worsen isolation unless directed by a clinician (NCRAR, 2024).

What audiology can do

  • Check hearing status when indicated.
  • Discuss sound tolerance, tinnitus, hyperacusis, and device concerns separately.
  • Coordinate with psychology, occupational therapy, school teams, or medical providers when appropriate.

When to seek urgent support

  • If triggers lead to thoughts of self-harm, violence, inability to attend school or work, or severe family conflict, seek urgent mental health or medical support.

Take-home message

Misophonia care should validate the reaction, protect safety, and build practical coping plans.
