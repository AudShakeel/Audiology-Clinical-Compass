Ototoxicity Awareness for Patients

Original counseling handoutPermission: host original textLast reviewed: May 11, 2026

Intended audience: Audiologists, oncology teams, hospitals

Patient population: Patients receiving ototoxic medications or with exposure concerns

Clinical purpose: Support monitoring and communication.

Key counseling points

  • Some medications and chemicals can affect hearing, tinnitus, or balance. Risk depends on drug, dose, treatment schedule, patient factors, and combined exposures (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, 2026) (World Health Organization, 2021).
  • Baseline and follow-up monitoring can help identify changes early and support communication between audiology, prescribing clinicians, and the patient (World Health Organization, 2021).
  • Patients should report new tinnitus, hearing change, dizziness, imbalance, or difficulty understanding speech during treatment (World Health Organization, 2021).

Clinician notes

Customize for local ototoxic monitoring protocols. Do not tell patients to stop medication without prescriber guidance.

Red flags and referral indicators

Urgent referral for sudden hearing change, severe dizziness, neurologic signs, or medication reaction concerns (U.S. Food and Drug Administration, 2022).

What happens next

Coordinate with prescribing clinician and document symptoms, audiogram changes, and communication impact.
