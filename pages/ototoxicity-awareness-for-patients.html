Ototoxicity Awareness for Patients

Original patient education handoutPrintable PDFLast reviewed: May 11, 2026

What this handout explains: Some medications, chemicals, or medical treatments can affect hearing, tinnitus, or balance. This is called ototoxicity.

Patient message: Do not stop, skip, or change prescribed medicine without speaking with the prescribing clinician.

Facts at a glance

  • Hearing and balance health can be affected by multiple medical and environmental factors (WHO, 2021).
  • Public health hearing care includes prevention, monitoring, and early identification of hearing problems (WHO, 2021) (CDC, 2026).
  • Medication decisions should always be handled by the prescribing medical provider, not changed independently because of this handout (FDA, 2022) .

What to watch for

  • New tinnitus.
  • Muffled hearing or trouble understanding speech.
  • New sound sensitivity.
  • Dizziness, imbalance, or unsteadiness.
  • Symptoms that start during or after a medication, chemotherapy, infection treatment, or toxic exposure.

What to tell your care team

  • Current medications and treatment schedule.
  • Prior hearing history.
  • Noise exposure history.
  • Whether symptoms are one-sided or both sides.
  • When symptoms began and whether they are changing.

What monitoring may include

  • Baseline and follow-up hearing tests.
  • High-frequency testing when available and clinically appropriate.
  • Tinnitus and balance symptom tracking.
  • Communication with oncology, infectious disease, primary care, occupational health, or other medical teams.

When to seek help

  • Report sudden hearing change, severe dizziness, falls, ear pain, drainage, or neurologic symptoms promptly (FDA, 2022) (NICE, 2023).

Take-home message

Ototoxicity care is about monitoring and communication, not changing medication without medical guidance.
