Classroom Accommodations for Hearing Loss

Original patient education handoutPrintable PDFLast reviewed: May 11, 2026

What this handout explains: Students with hearing loss may need accommodations even when they use hearing aids, cochlear implants, bone-conduction devices, or remote microphones.

Patient message: Accommodations are not special favors. They are access tools.

Facts at a glance

  • Children who are deaf or hard of hearing need access that supports language, literacy, and psychosocial development (JCIH, 2019).
  • Classroom acoustic access may require remote microphone technology, seating, teacher awareness, device checks, and communication supports (American Academy of Audiology, 2008) (American Academy of Audiology, 2013).
  • Auditory processing and listening concerns should be considered with educational, language, and attention factors (ASHA, 2024) .

Common accommodations

  • Preferential seating with visual access to the teacher and board.
  • Remote microphone or hearing assistance technology when appropriate.
  • Written directions and key vocabulary.
  • Captioned videos.
  • Reduced background noise when possible.
  • Check for understanding without embarrassing the student.

Device access routines

  • Daily device check at home or school.
  • Back-up batteries, charger access, and storage plan.
  • Staff know how to mute, charge, and troubleshoot remote microphones.
  • Plan should specify who is responsible for equipment checks (American Academy of Audiology, 2008).

Student self-advocacy

  • Teach the student age-appropriate phrases such as, 'Please face me,' 'The microphone is off,' or 'Can you repeat the last direction?'.
  • Let the student help identify what works and what feels uncomfortable.
  • Track fatigue, missed instructions, and social participation.

When to update the plan

  • New hearing results.
  • New classroom or teacher.
  • Change in device use, grades, behavior, fatigue, or social participation.
  • Repeated equipment problems or remote microphone non-use.

Take-home message

A good classroom plan makes listening access predictable, visible, and shared by the team.
