Congenital Cytomegalovirus and Hearing Loss

Original patient education handoutPrintable PDFLast reviewed: May 11, 2026

What this handout explains: Congenital cytomegalovirus, or congenital CMV, is an infection present at birth that can be associated with hearing loss. Hearing loss may be present at birth or develop later.

Patient message: A baby can pass newborn hearing screening and still develop hearing loss later. Monitoring matters.

Facts at a glance

  • CDC states that congenital CMV-related hearing loss can occur at birth or develop later, and one ear can be affected before the other (CDC, 2024).
  • Audiology organizations support early identification and monitoring of infants with congenital CMV and hearing loss risk (American Academy of Audiology, 2023) (AAO-HNS, 2025).
  • Children with hearing loss need timely intervention to support communication and development (JCIH, 2019) .

Hearing features

  • Hearing loss may be unilateral or bilateral.
  • It may be mild to profound.
  • It may fluctuate, progress, or appear later in childhood.
  • Regular audiology follow-up is important when congenital CMV is known or suspected.

What families can do

  • Keep all recommended hearing follow-up visits.
  • Watch for changes in response to sound, speech, startle, localization, or listening behavior.
  • Share CMV history with audiology, ENT, pediatrics, early intervention, and school teams.

Medical care

  • Antiviral treatment decisions, lab testing, and medical monitoring belong with the medical team.
  • Audiology should coordinate hearing results with pediatric and specialty care.

When to seek help

  • Sudden hearing change, new speech delay, loss of sound responses, ear symptoms, balance concerns, or device non-benefit should be reported promptly.

Take-home message

Congenital CMV hearing care requires ongoing monitoring because hearing can change after birth.
