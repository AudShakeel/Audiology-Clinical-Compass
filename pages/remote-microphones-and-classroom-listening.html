Remote Microphones and Classroom Listening

Original patient education handoutPrintable PDFLast reviewed: May 11, 2026

What this handout explains: Remote microphones place the microphone near the talker, which can improve access to speech when distance, noise, or reverberation makes listening difficult.

Patient message: A remote microphone does not replace hearing aids, cochlear implants, classroom supports, or good communication habits. It improves the signal that reaches the device.

Facts at a glance

  • Remote microphone technology is used to improve the speech signal for children and youth in complex listening settings (American Academy of Audiology, 2008).
  • Classroom listening can be affected by distance, background noise, reverberation, attention demands, and auditory processing needs (ASHA, 2024) (American Academy of Audiology, 2008).
  • Adults may also benefit from remote microphones in meetings, lectures, places of worship, and group settings (ASHA, 2024) (NICE, 2023) .

When it may help

  • The teacher, speaker, or group leader is far away.
  • The room is noisy or echoey.
  • The listener misses instructions despite wearing devices.
  • Listening difficulty is present with hearing loss, cochlear implants, auditory processing concerns, or other access needs (American Academy of Audiology, 2008) (ASHA, 2024).

How to use it well

  • Place the microphone close to the main talker.
  • Mute during private conversations or when not teaching.
  • Check connection before class, meetings, or events.
  • Teach the student or adult user how to report when the microphone is not working.

School team reminders

  • Access plans should include device checks, backup procedures, teacher education, and student self-advocacy (American Academy of Audiology, 2008) (JCIH, 2019).
  • Document who charges, stores, checks, and troubleshoots the system.
  • Review benefit with functional listening measures and real-world feedback when possible.

When to ask for help

  • Sound is intermittent, too soft, delayed, distorted, or not connecting.
  • The student reports hearing everyone except the teacher.
  • Classroom performance or listening fatigue worsens despite consistent use.

Take-home message

Remote microphones are most useful when the microphone is close to the speaker and daily checks are routine.
