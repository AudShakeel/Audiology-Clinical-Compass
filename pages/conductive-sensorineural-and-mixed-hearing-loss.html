Conductive, Sensorineural, and Mixed Hearing Loss

Original counseling handoutPermission: host original textLast reviewed: May 11, 2026

Intended audience: Adults, parents, trainees

Patient population: Patients reviewing audiologic results

Clinical purpose: Give a simple explanation of hearing-loss type.

Original simplified counseling visual. Verify patient-specific results on the clinical audiogram.

Key counseling points

  • Conductive loss means sound is reduced before it reaches the inner ear. Sensorineural loss usually involves the cochlea or auditory nerve. Mixed loss includes both components (National Institute for Health and Care Excellence, 2023) (World Health Organization, 2021).
  • The type of hearing loss guides referral, medical evaluation, amplification decisions, and follow-up. Audiologists should not treat an unexplained medical pattern as a routine device-only problem (National Institute for Health and Care Excellence, 2023) (U.S. Food and Drug Administration, 2022).
  • A bone-conduction or middle-ear finding may require different counseling than a typical sloping sensorineural loss (National Institute for Health and Care Excellence, 2023).

Clinician notes

Use with audiogram explanation and tympanometry. Document whether the loss is conductive, sensorineural, mixed, unilateral, bilateral, symmetric, fluctuating, or progressive.

Red flags and referral indicators

Refer for drainage, pain, sudden hearing change, asymmetric/unilateral concerns, abnormal otoscopy, or unexplained conductive components (U.S. Food and Drug Administration, 2022) (National Institute for Health and Care Excellence, 2023).

What happens next

Discuss ENT referral when medically indicated, hearing technology candidacy, and follow-up testing.
