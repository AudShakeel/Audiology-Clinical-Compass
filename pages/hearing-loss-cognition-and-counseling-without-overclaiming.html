Hearing Loss, Cognition, and Counseling Without Overclaiming

Original counseling handoutPermission: host original textLast reviewed: May 11, 2026

Intended audience: Audiologists, adult patients, families

Patient population: Adults concerned about cognitive health and hearing loss

Clinical purpose: Support cautious counseling about association and intervention.

Key counseling points

  • Hearing loss is associated with cognitive and social outcomes, but clinicians should avoid claiming that hearing aids prevent dementia for every patient (National Institute for Health and Care Excellence, 2023) (Livingston et al., 2024).
  • The ACHIEVE trial found that hearing intervention did not reduce 3-year cognitive decline in the full cohort, but did reduce decline in a prespecified higher-risk older adult subgroup (Lin et al., 2023).
  • A clinically accurate message is that hearing care can improve communication access, participation, safety, and listening effort, while cognitive-health claims should be presented cautiously (National Institute for Health and Care Excellence, 2023) (Lin et al., 2023).

Clinician notes

Use when patients ask about dementia. Do not use fear-based sales language.

Red flags and referral indicators

Refer for sudden cognitive changes, safety concerns, depression, neurologic symptoms, or suspected delirium through appropriate medical pathways.

What happens next

Document counseling wording and patient goals around communication, participation, and quality of life.
