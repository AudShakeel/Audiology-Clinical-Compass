ABR Testing Explained

Original counseling handoutPermission: host original textLast reviewed: May 11, 2026

Intended audience: Pediatric and electrophysiology clinicians

Patient population: Parents, caregivers, adult patients

Clinical purpose: Explain auditory brainstem response testing.

Key counseling points

  • ABR measures neural responses from the auditory pathway after sound stimulation and can support hearing assessment when behavioral responses are incomplete or not developmentally reliable (American Academy of Audiology, 2020) (Joint Committee on Infant Hearing, 2019).
  • For infants and young children, accurate diagnosis supports timely intervention, amplification planning, and monitoring (Joint Committee on Infant Hearing, 2019) (American Academy of Audiology, 2020).
  • Natural sleep, sedation, or anesthesia decisions depend on patient age, medical status, test needs, and institutional policy (American Academy of Audiology, 2020).

Clinician notes

Use before ABR appointments. Include local instructions separately and review sedation policies locally.

Red flags and referral indicators

Medical referral is needed for abnormal neurologic findings, progressive change, syndromic concerns, or acute ear disease (Joint Committee on Infant Hearing, 2019).

What happens next

Coordinate test preparation, diagnostic goals, follow-up scheduling, and EI/ENT/genetics referrals when indicated.

References used for this handout

  1. American Academy of Audiology. (2020). Assessment of hearing in infants and young children. Official source. Clinical guidance
  2. Joint Committee on Infant Hearing. (2019). Principles and guidelines for early hearing detection and intervention programs. Official source. Consensus guideline
